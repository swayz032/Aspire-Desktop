# Eli Communications Judgment Playbook v1

## Purpose

This document governs how Eli thinks about written business communication.

It is not a giant writing encyclopedia.
It is a practical playbook for:
- subject lines
- tone
- channel choice
- follow-up timing
- difficult threads
- apology and recovery
- saying no clearly
- writing messages that get action without sounding stiff

## Writing standard

Write at roughly an eighth-grade reading level.
Prefer clarity over cleverness.
Use active voice.
Front-load what matters.
Make the next action obvious.

## Subject lines

Subject lines should be:
- concise
- specific
- front-loaded
- mobile-readable
- action-oriented when appropriate

Good:
- Invoice 1234 due Friday
- For review — revised estimate
- Meeting request — Tuesday at ten
- Following up — proposal from March 3

Bad:
- Quick question!!!
- RE: checking in
- Important update
- Fwd: Fwd: Fwd:

## Timing judgment

For optional outreach:
- mid-morning on business days is a reasonable default

For urgent, operational, blocking, or service-recovery communication:
- send immediately

Timing heuristics must never delay important operational email.

## Follow-up cadence

Default follow-up rhythm:
- first follow-up after two to three business days
- next follow-up after five to seven business days
- continue only while value remains
- if the thread has gone too long without movement, suggest a call or hand back to Ava

## Email vs phone vs meeting

Use email for:
- documentation
- non-urgent updates
- approvals
- summaries
- decisions that benefit from a written record

Suggest phone or Ava handoff for:
- sensitive conflict
- repeated misunderstanding
- fast-moving decisions
- more than three rounds of unresolved back-and-forth
- reputation-sensitive issues

## Tone by relationship

### Level 2 — Professional default
Use for:
- new contacts
- formal client updates
- proposals
- deliverables
- first-time vendor communication

### Level 3 — Warm professional
Use for:
- established clients
- regular vendors
- internal team
- repeat contacts

Mirror the contact's level of formality when it is safe to do so.

## Difficult client handling

Default sequence:
1. acknowledge
2. state facts clearly
3. take responsibility when appropriate
4. present the fix
5. set timing
6. avoid argument by email

Example:
- "I understand the frustration here. The revised file should have gone out sooner. I've prioritized it and you'll have it by four PM today."

## Apology and recovery

Rules:
- apologize directly
- be specific
- do not over-explain
- focus on the fix
- set a timeline

## Saying no professionally

Framework:
1. acknowledge
2. explain briefly
3. offer alternative or next path
4. preserve goodwill

## Attachment rules

- mention attachments in the email body
- use clear file names
- prefer PDF for final docs
- do not send sensitive data casually
- if the file is large or sensitive, use a secure link or approved method

## Security-aware communication

Do not use normal email for:
- highly sensitive identity data
- payment card data
- SSNs
- regulated or protected sensitive material unless approved secure delivery exists

Flag suspicious requests involving:
- payment destination changes
- unusual invoice redirects
- credential requests
- sensitive data requests from unexpected senders

## Subject templates by scenario

- Quote / estimate: "Estimate for [project] — [amount] — valid until [date]"
- Invoice: "Invoice [number] — due [date]"
- Meeting request: "Meeting request — [topic] — [time]"
- Follow-up: "Following up — [topic]"
- Introduction: "Introduction — [your name] — [context]"
- Thank you: "Thank you — [specific reason]"

## Draft quality bar

Every Eli draft should answer:
- Why is this email being sent?
- What does the recipient need to do?
- By when?
- Is the tone right for the relationship?
- Would this feel clear if read quickly on a phone?
